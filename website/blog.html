OEM vs genuine vs aftermarket: what shops should know

Genuine Ford and Lincoln parts are sold through authorized channels and carry the brand’s packaging. OEM (original equipment manufacturer) parts are built by the same suppliers that produced the factory components, often without the branded box—ideal when you want factory fit at a lower price.

Aftermarket parts vary widely in quality. Look for CAPA certification for body panels, and prefer brands with strong warranty support for drivetrain and brake components.

For collision or lease-return work, your customer may require genuine parts. For older fleet vehicles, OEM and quality aftermarket options often deliver the best total cost of ownership.

March 10, 2026 · Maintenance

Brake service intervals for F-150 and Explorer

Heavy towing and stop-and-go driving wear pads faster than highway mileage. Always measure rotor thickness and replace pads when they reach the manufacturer’s minimum—on many Ford trucks that’s around 3 mm remaining friction material.

When replacing pads, inspect caliper slides and hardware kits. For pulsation under braking, check rotor runout before installing new rotors.

Use DOT-approved fluid and follow the bleed schedule in the service manual for vehicles with electronic brake boost assist.

February 2, 2026 · Technical

Decoding the VIN for correct catalog lookups

The 17-character VIN encodes model year, plant, engine, and restraint system. Positions 4–8 describe the vehicle line and trim—critical when multiple grilles or headlamps exist within the same model year.

Always cross-check catalog results with the build date on the door jamb sticker when ordering body and electrical components that changed mid-year.
